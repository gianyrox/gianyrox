## 1. Medium Prologue Post (1,200–1,500 words)

**Title: Pablo's Mother Almost Died Because a Bank in Miami Closed at 5 p.m.**

The first time I understood that money was broken, I was standing on a rooftop in Buenos Aires watching a man named Pablo cry into his phone.

It was a Tuesday. His mother, in Bogotá, needed $300 for a chemotherapy infusion scheduled the next morning. Pablo had the money. The money was in a Wells Fargo account in Miami. The infusion clinic, in Colombia, needed pesos in a bank account by 8 a.m. Wednesday.

This is the part of the story where, if you've ever lived between two countries, your stomach already knows the ending.

The wire didn't arrive. Not Wednesday. Not Thursday. The clinic moved Pablo's mother to the back of the queue. The pharmacist refused to release the drugs without payment confirmation. Pablo's sister sat in a plastic chair in a Bogotá hallway for sixteen hours, watching her mother's IV drip saline because the chemo couldn't be hung without a receipt.

The wire finally cleared on Friday afternoon, four days after Pablo sent it. The bank in Miami had charged him $25. The intermediary bank — a Citibank correspondent he had never heard of — had taken another $15. The receiving bank in Bogotá had quietly applied a 6% markdown to the exchange rate, which on $300 is another $18.

Pablo's mother got her chemo. She lived. But Pablo lost something that week that he never quite got back: the belief that the financial system he had been told was the most advanced in the world was, in fact, advanced.

Three months later, his cousin in Medellín showed him a wallet on his phone called Phantom. Pablo loaded $300 of USDC — a digital dollar issued by a company called Circle, backed one-to-one by U.S. Treasuries — and sent it to his mother's wallet. The transaction confirmed in 90 seconds. The total cost was $1.20. His mother walked to a corner store in Chapinero, scanned a QR code, and walked out with 1,180,000 Colombian pesos in cash.

Pablo did not become a crypto-bro. He did not buy Bitcoin. He did not read a white paper. He did not learn what a Merkle tree is. He learned one thing, and one thing only: **there is a faster pipe, and the people who control the slow pipe are not going to tell you about it.**

---

I have spent the last two years on that rooftop, and on a hundred others like it, in Lagos and Harare and Bangkok and Istanbul and Mexico City, watching ordinary people quietly walk off the rails of the international banking system and onto something else.

The something else does not have a name yet that anyone agrees on. The trade press calls it "stablecoins." Central banks call it "private digital currency." Treasury calls it "tokenized dollar liabilities." Pablo's mother calls it "the new way Pablito sends the medicine money."

Whatever you call it, $11.3 trillion of it moved across blockchain rails in 2025. That is more than Visa. That is roughly the same volume as Mastercard. And almost none of the people moving it — the Mercys, the Femis, the Pablos — would describe themselves as crypto investors. They would describe themselves as people who needed to move money and found a faster door.

This essay is the prologue to a book I just finished writing called **Your Money Is Broken: How Stablecoins, the Digital Dollar, and Cross-Border Payments Are Replacing Slow Money**. The book is not a manifesto. It is not a prediction. It is a field report. I went to the places where the old pipes are visibly cracking and I wrote down what I saw.

What I saw is this:

**In Harare**, a 44-year-old woman named Mercy runs a savings circle — what Zimbabweans call a *chama* — for fourteen women who pool $40 each every Sunday afternoon. The Zimbabwean dollar lost 56% of its value last year. Mercy used to keep the pot in cash, in a biscuit tin, under her bed. She would watch the tin shrink in real terms every week. Now she converts the pot into USDT (a different digital dollar, this one from a company called Tether) the same afternoon they collect it. When a member needs cash — for a funeral, for school fees, for a clinic visit — Mercy converts back. The pot held its value last year. The biscuit tin would have lost about $250.

**In Lagos**, a textile importer named Femi nearly lost a $100,000 supplier contract from a factory in Shenzhen because Nigerian banks could not process his outbound wire within the 72-hour quote window the supplier had given him. The CBN dollar window was closed. The parallel market spread was 14%. Femi paid the invoice in USDC, on-chain, in eleven minutes. The Shenzhen factory had a wallet because half their Nigerian customers now do. Nobody on either side of that transaction thought of themselves as a "crypto user." They thought of themselves as a textile importer and a textile exporter trying to close a deal before Friday.

**In Buenos Aires**, where the peso has lost roughly 90% of its value in two years, an entire shadow dollar economy now runs on USDT. The price of an apartment is quoted in dollars. The dollars are increasingly digital. The notary, last time I bought something, accepted a wallet transfer in the lobby of his office while we waited for a coffee.

None of these people are ideological. None of them care about decentralization, or self-custody, or the long arc of monetary history. They care about whether the money moves. The money moves. So they use the thing that moves.

---

The story the financial press tells is that "crypto failed" — that the speculative casino of 2021 collapsed and the believers are licking their wounds. That story is, in the narrow sense, true. NFTs are dead. Most tokens are worthless. The pump-and-dumps deserved to die.

But while that fire was burning down the front of the building, a completely different thing was being built, quietly, in the back. The back of the building is just a payment rail. A boring, useful, $11-trillion-a-year payment rail that happens to be open 24/7, settles in seconds, costs a few cents, and does not require permission from a correspondent bank in Miami that closes at 5 p.m. on a Tuesday.

The book is about that back of the building. It is anti-maximalist. It is anti-hype. It does not predict that Bitcoin will hit a million dollars, because I do not know and neither does anyone else. It predicts something much smaller and much more boring: **that the rails by which $300 leaves Miami and arrives in Bogotá are being quietly replaced, from below, by people who have no idea they are part of a revolution, because for them it is not a revolution. It is a Tuesday afternoon and the medicine needs to arrive by Wednesday morning.**

If you have ever waited four days for a wire, paid $40 to send $300, watched a 6% FX haircut disappear into a bank you have never heard of, or lain awake at 3 a.m. wondering if the money got through — this book is for you.

## 2. Substack First Post (800–1,000 words)

**Title: Dispatches From the Digital Dollar Economy — A Note From the First Issue**

I'm starting this Substack because I just spent two years writing a book about something I cannot stop thinking about, and a book — even a short one — is a finished object. It closes. The thing the book is about does not close. It moves every Tuesday afternoon when Pablo's mother needs her chemo money, and every Sunday evening when Mercy's chama converts a pot of Zimbabwean dollars into USDT, and every Friday when Femi wires $100,000 to a textile factory in Shenzhen because his Nigerian bank can't.

So this Substack will be the open version of the book. Dispatches. Field notes. Things I saw last week. Things I got wrong in the manuscript and want to correct in public. Things readers have written to me about that I did not know.

A few ground rules, so you know what you're signing up for:

**1. This is not a crypto Substack.**

I do not care what Bitcoin does this week. I do not have a price target. I do not run a fund. I will never tell you to buy a token. If you want that, there are seventeen excellent newsletters and I am not one of them. What I care about is the boring infrastructure underneath — the actual rails that actual dollars are actually flowing across, because they happen to be cheaper and faster than the rails your bank uses. The fact that those rails were built by the same industry that produced Dogecoin is an accident of history. The rails work anyway.

**2. The characters are real. The numbers are real. The names are sometimes changed.**

In the book, you meet Pablo, who lost four days of his mother's cancer treatment to a SWIFT wire. You meet Mercy, who runs a fourteen-woman savings circle in Harare and parks the pot in Tether. You meet Femi, who saved a $100K textile order with an eleven-minute on-chain transfer. These people exist. I have met them. I have eaten their food and ridden in their cars and watched them open their phones to show me wallets with more discipline than most American retail investors. Sometimes I change their names. I never change their numbers.

**3. I am skeptical of every side of this.**

I am skeptical of the Bitcoin maximalists, who think the answer is a 21-million-coin gold-standard cosplay. I am skeptical of the central bankers, who think they can build a CBDC that anyone outside their own treasury department will voluntarily use. I am skeptical of the stablecoin issuers themselves — Tether's reserves are not the bedrock of a global financial system, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling you something. I am most skeptical of the people who tell me this is all going to be fine.

And yet: $11.3 trillion moved across stablecoin rails in 2025. More than Visa. About the same as Mastercard. That is not a thought experiment. That is the system telling you what it is, whether or not the people in charge of the system have caught up.

---

The first dispatch, which will land in your inbox next Tuesday, is about an unmarked office in a strip mall in Mexico City where you can walk in with a USB drive and walk out with a stack of cash. The office does $40 million a month. It is staffed by two women, one of whom has a baby in a sling. There is no sign. There is no website. There is a Telegram channel and a queue of people on Calle Florencia every morning at nine. This is what "on-ramp" and "off-ramp" actually look like in the places where the financial system has already left.

The second dispatch will be a long piece on what the Argentine peso has done to the people I love, and why every café in Palermo now has a sign that quietly says *acepto USDT.*

The third will be a piece on the thing I got most wrong in the book — which is that I underestimated, by roughly a factor of four, how much of West African e-commerce is now settling on-chain. I have been on the phone with three Lagos founders trying to recalibrate.

After that, who knows. The point of a Substack is to find out together.

## 4. LinkedIn Launch Post (250–350 words)

The dominant story about crypto over the last three years has been a story about failure: collapsed exchanges, worthless tokens, wrecked retail speculators. In the narrow sense, that story is true.

But underneath the speculative wreckage, a quieter thing has been happening: the gradual, unsentimental rebuilding of cross-border payment rails from below.

In 2025, roughly $11.3 trillion moved across stablecoin rails — comparable to Visa's annual volume, and approaching Mastercard's. Almost none of that volume came from the speculative side of the industry. It came from importers settling supplier invoices, remittance senders supporting family abroad, freelancers receiving payment from foreign clients, and savers in inflationary economies trying to preserve purchasing power.

The infrastructure that the financial press dismissed as a casino is, increasingly, a payments utility. It is open 24/7. It settles in seconds. It costs cents per transaction rather than tens of dollars. And — most importantly — it does not require permission from a correspondent banking system whose architecture has not been meaningfully upgraded since the 1970s.

I just published *Your Money Is Broken: How Stablecoins, the Digital Dollar, and Cross-Border Payments Are Replacing Slow Money* — a field report on what this rail rebuild actually looks like at the level of the people using it: a textile importer in Lagos who saved a $100K deal because the new rail closed faster than his bank, a savings-club organizer in Harare hedging 56% inflation with tokenized dollars, a son in Miami whose monthly remittance to his cancer-stricken mother dropped from a 4-day, $43 transaction to a 90-second, $1.20 transaction.
